The Problem in Detail

Why the wet lab is the wrong first step in modern discovery.

The argument for in-silico-first drug discovery is not that wet-lab work is unnecessary — wet-lab confirmation remains essential for any asset that will be synthesised. The argument is that wet-lab work at the full library scale is economically irrational when computational triage at ten thousand to one million compound scale can reduce the library to a tractable one to five per cent at which wet-lab investment is productive.

1
The traditional workflow and its cost structure
What Indian pharma is spending today on early discovery

A typical early-stage discovery programme at an Indian pharmaceutical company targeting a novel or validated biological target proceeds through several sequential wet-lab stages, each of which consumes significant capital, time, and scientific attention. Understanding the unit economics of the traditional pathway clarifies where computational substitution offers the largest marginal returns.

This is precisely the work that modern computational chemistry with graphical processing unit acceleration performs with thirty to a thousand times cost advantage and five to fifty times speed advantage, delivering a prioritised ranked list rather than an exhaustive enumeration.

2
Why ADMET failure is particularly expensive
The asymmetry between in-silico prediction and in-vivo surprise

A molecule that fails its ADMET profile at the in-vitro assay stage has cost the sponsor Rs 2–5 lakh in assay spend plus approximately Rs 75,000–2 lakh in prior screening and synthesis. A molecule that fails its ADMET profile in human clinical trial — because the preclinical assay panel was incomplete, or because the species translation was imperfect, or simply because the compound class interacts with the CYP3A4 or hERG channel in ways the preclinical panel did not detect — has cost the sponsor between Rs 40 crore and Rs 200 crore depending on the phase of failure.

Wong et al. (Biostatistics, 2019) analysed 21,143 clinical development pathways between 2000 and 2015 and reported ADMET-related failures as the single largest category of Phase II and Phase III clinical failures, outranking efficacy failure. Subsequent analyses (Harrison 2016, Thomas et al. 2021) refine this figure to the 30–45% range depending on therapeutic area and failure classification methodology. For small-molecule oral drug programmes in central nervous system, oncology, and cardiovascular indications, the proportion is at the upper end of this range.

Modern graph neural network architectures — ChemProp, Attentive Fingerprint, MPNN, Graph Convolutional Network — trained on the Therapeutics Data Commons benchmarks and validated against DrugBank and ChEMBL, achieve receiver-operating-characteristic area under the curve between 0.80 and 0.92 for most ADMET endpoints. This is substantially below wet-lab gold standard for any individual compound, but at the triage scale (eliminate the bottom fifty per cent of the library before expensive wet-lab work) it is transformatively valuable.

Why Indian pharma has not built this capability in-house
The scale mismatch that creates our business opportunity

Building an in-house computational chemistry team capable of running a full discovery support function requires, at minimum, four to six doctorate-level scientists (structural bioinformatics, molecular modeling, machine learning, cheminformatics), two to three computational biologists or bioinformatics engineers, a graphical-processing-unit cluster of minimum twenty teraflops per second accuracy at sixteen-bit floating point precision, and a licensing budget of Rs 30–80 lakh per year for commercial packages (Schrödinger Maestro suite, OpenEye, Biovia Discovery Studio) where required. Total fully-loaded annual cost: Rs 2–3.5 crore.

For an Indian pharmaceutical firm running three to ten discovery programmes per year, each of which might consume Rs 5–20 lakh of computational work, the annualised cost-per- programme of building this capability in-house becomes prohibitive unless the firm operates at the scale of the top ten Indian innovator companies. Below that scale, outsourced computational services scale with demand, pay only for what is used, and gain access to a specialist team that amortises its cost across multiple sponsors.

This is precisely the market structure — a large number of small-to-medium sponsors each with episodic need for specialist compute and scientific expertise — that supports a services-based business model. The parallel in the industry is the success of contract research organisations in preclinical biology (Syngene, Aragen, Veeda) built on the same economic logic applied to wet-lab work.

01
Stage 1 · Target acquisition and preparation
Structural biology prerequisites for rational design

Every structure-based drug design project begins with a protein structure. Where the sponsor provides a crystallographically resolved structure of the target in the desired conformational state, we proceed directly to preparation. Where the target has no resolved structure, or where the relevant conformational state (apo, holo, allosteric modulator-bound) is not available in the Protein Data Bank, we generate a high-confidence model.

Tools and methods

  • AlphaFold2 (DeepMind, Nature 2021) for de novo structure prediction. We run the full AlphaFold2 pipeline locally on our graphical- processing-unit cluster including MSA generation, template search, and structure relaxation. Per-residue pLDDT (predicted local-distance difference test) confidence scores are retained and reported to the customer to support downstream interpretation.
  • ESMFold (Meta FAIR, Science 2023) for rapid structure prediction in cases where AlphaFold2 runtime is prohibitive, or for orthogonal validation. ESMFold typically runs one to two orders of magnitude faster than AlphaFold2 at modest accuracy cost for well-represented fold classes.
  • Fpocket and custom pocket-volume analysis for binding site identification, including allosteric sites and cryptic pockets identified through molecular dynamics ensemble analysis.
  • PDBFixer and custom protonation-state assignment (PROPKA, H++) for the atomic-level preparation required before docking or molecular dynamics.
  • AMBER ff14SB / ff19SB force fields for protein parameterisation in molecular dynamics contexts; GAFF2 or OpenFF Sage for ligand parameterisation.

Deliverable format

At the conclusion of Stage 1 the customer receives: the prepared protein structure file (PDB), residue-level confidence analysis for any predicted components, identified binding site locations with volumetric characterisation, suggested docking grid specifications, and a brief methodology statement suitable for inclusion in internal discovery project documentation or regulatory filings.

02
Stage 2 · Library preparation
Chemical-space management at 10⁴–10⁶ scale

Effective virtual screening depends on proper preparation of the compound library at the three-dimensional structure level. A compound submitted as a SMILES string without explicit protonation, tautomer, or stereoisomer enumeration may miss the biologically relevant form entirely. Library preparation is the stage that most frequently differentiates a production-grade pipeline from a demonstration-grade pipeline.

Library sources we support

  • Customer-provided libraries in SMILES, SDF, or MOL2 format — typical scale 10³–10⁲ compounds representing internal compound collections, historic hits, or focused libraries around a scaffold of interest.
  • ZINC20 (Irwin and Shoichet, Journal of Chemical Information and Modeling 2020) providing approximately 1.4 billion purchasable compounds with documented vendor availability. We support Lipinski-filtered, lead-like, and fragment subsets.
  • Enamine REAL Space providing approximately 48 billion make-on-demand molecules from a documented 140-step reaction library with 80-90% synthesis success rate. Cost-per-compound for make-on-demand synthesis typically Rs 10,000–50,000 versus Rs 100,000–500,000 for bespoke synthesis.
  • ChEMBL for target-focused libraries built around known active compounds for the target or related targets; useful for scaffold-hopping exercises.
  • Curated natural-product libraries (COCONUT, NPASS) for sponsors with an interest in natural-product-inspired design.

Preparation pipeline

  • Protonation state enumeration at physiological pH (6.8–8.0) using Dimorphite-DL or Epik-equivalent methodology.
  • Tautomer enumeration retaining canonical, keto, enol, and relevant prototropic forms within energy threshold typically 5 kcal/mol.
  • Stereoisomer enumeration for unspecified stereocenters; retention of customer-specified stereochemistry where provided.
  • 3D conformer generation via RDKit's ETKDGv3 or OMEGA where licensed, typically one to fifty low-energy conformers per compound depending on flexibility.
  • Lipinski / Veber / Ghose filter application where customer specifies lead-like or drug-like subsetting.
03
Stage 3 · High-throughput virtual screening
Scoring function selection and rescoring strategy

Molecular docking is the conventional entry point for structure-based virtual screening. Modern best practice acknowledges that any single docking scoring function is imperfect and that consensus scoring — running multiple orthogonal scoring functions and combining their outputs — provides materially better enrichment than reliance on a single score.

Docking engines deployed

  • AutoDock Vina (Trott and Olson 2010; Eberhardt et al. 2021): baseline empirical scoring, typical throughput approximately 1,000 compounds per central-processing- unit-core-hour. Deployed as primary docking engine for majority of projects.
  • Smina (Koes et al. 2013): fork of AutoDock Vina with improved scoring function options and custom flexibility handling.
  • GNINA (Ragoza et al. 2017; McNutt et al. 2021): convolutional-neural- network rescoring on top of Vina pose generation. Typically provides 15–25% enrichment improvement over Vina alone on DUD-E targets but at 5–10x compute cost.
  • DiffDock (Corso et al. 2023): diffusion-model pose prediction for orthogonal validation of top hits from conventional docking.
  • Glide (Schrödinger) deployable via customer-licensed pathway for projects where regulatory or historical consistency requires commercial scoring.

Consensus rescoring strategy

Top one per cent of Vina-ranked compounds are subjected to GNINA rescoring. Top 0.1% of the consensus-ranked list proceeds to MM-GBSA single-point rescoring using a brief energy minimisation in explicit or implicit solvent. This staged rescoring captures the throughput advantage of fast empirical scoring while using higher-accuracy but slower methods on the reduced list where they actually change decisions.

Benchmarking

We benchmark our virtual-screening pipeline against DUD-E (Directory of Useful Decoys, Enhanced; Mysinger et al. 2012), a community-standard benchmark set covering one hundred and two protein targets with validated actives and property-matched decoys. Typical performance of our pipeline achieves enrichment factor at 1% in the range of 15–45 depending on target, consistent with published literature for the underlying tools. We publish per-project benchmark results on request.

Stage 5 · Molecular dynamics validation
Physics-based confirmation of top-ranked candidates

Docking scoring functions are approximations. They do not capture conformational entropy, explicit water-mediated interactions, or the full thermodynamics of binding. Molecular dynamics simulation — integrating the Newtonian equations of motion for every atom in the protein-ligand-solvent system over nanosecond-to-microsecond timescales — provides physics-based confirmation that cannot be matched by pure scoring-function ranking.

Our MD workflow

  • GROMACS 2024 as primary molecular dynamics engine. Single-node central-processing-unit performance approximately 100–300 nanoseconds per day for 50,000-atom systems; graphical-processing-unit-accelerated performance approximately 300–800 nanoseconds per day depending on system size.
  • OpenMM 8.x for Python-scriptable custom simulations, enhanced sampling implementations (metadynamics, replica exchange), and free-energy perturbation calculations.
  • AMBER ff19SB force field for proteins, GAFF2 or OpenFF Sage 2.1.0 for small molecules, TIP3P or OPC water model.
  • 100–500 nanoseconds production simulation length for binding-mode stability assessment on top five to twenty candidates per project.
  • MM-GBSA / MM-PBSA free-energy-of-binding rescoring via gmx_MMPBSA or in-house scripts. While absolute MM-GBSA values are not quantitatively accurate for absolute free energies, relative rankings within a congeneric series correlate reasonably with experimental affinity (Spearman ρ typically 0.4–0.7).
  • Thermodynamic integration or FEP+ for high-value lead-optimisation projects where customer licenses Schrödinger FEP+ or supports GROMACS free-energy calculation workflows.

Analysis outputs

Molecular dynamics analysis deliverables include: root-mean-square deviation trajectories of ligand and binding-site residues, hydrogen-bond occupancy analysis across the simulation, water-bridge identification, per-residue interaction energy decomposition, and binding-mode cluster analysis to distinguish stably-bound candidates from those that drift or unbind.

01
Anomaly Detection · detailed architecture
Unsupervised detection of deviation from learned normal behavior

The anomaly detection model is deployed against three distinct data modalities: network-flow records aggregated at five-second windows, endpoint process-event streams, and authentication log sequences. Each modality uses a tailored architecture rather than a one-size-fits-all model. This is consistent with academic and industry best practice where unsupervised anomaly detection performance degrades rapidly when architectures are applied outside their native data type.

Network-flow anomaly architecture

A variational autoencoder with an encoder compressing 40-dimensional flow features (source / destination IP as hashed embeddings, port entropy, packet-size distribution, inter-arrival time statistics, protocol-flag frequencies, bytes-transferred distributions) to a 12-dimensional latent space. Reconstruction error thresholded at the 99.5th percentile of training-set error to raise anomaly alerts. Threshold adapted monthly via rolling-window calibration against ground-truth labels provided by customer SOC team feedback.

Endpoint process anomaly architecture

An isolation forest operating on sequential process-event features: parent-child process chains, command-line argument patterns, registry-access fingerprints, file-system modification sequences. Isolation depth-based scoring identifies outliers without requiring labelled malicious examples. Ensemble with a denoising autoencoder for robustness against adversarial process-tree construction attacks.

Authentication sequence anomaly architecture

A long short-term memory (LSTM) encoder processing sequences of authentication events per user. The model learns a user-specific normal activity pattern and raises anomaly alerts when the next-event probability under the learned model falls below calibrated threshold. Handles account-takeover and session-hijacking signals that do not manifest in any individual event but become apparent across sequence patterns.

Training data and retraining cadence

Anomaly models are trained per customer on the customer's own normal-behavior baseline. This avoids the noise and drift problems of cross-customer anomaly models that plague generic anomaly-detection vendors. Baseline is established on the first four to six weeks of customer deployment, then refined monthly with concept-drift detection algorithms (ADWIN, Page-Hinkley). Retraining cadence: monthly full retrain plus continuous online adaptation for slow drift.

Expected performance

In published industry benchmarks and academic literature on similar architectures (Liu, Nguyen, et al. 2023; Garcia et al. 2014 CTU-13 dataset; UNSW-NB15 dataset), well-calibrated anomaly detection systems achieve true positive rates in the range 0.75–0.90 at false-positive rates of 0.01–0.03. Production performance depends substantially on customer environment stability and labelled-feedback quality. We disclose measured performance per customer deployment as part of regular reporting.

Classification · detailed architecture
Supervised categorisation across known threat types

The classification model ensemble targets four distinct classification tasks. We do not train a single monolithic classifier because the optimal architecture differs materially across modalities, and because performance monitoring is clearer when each task has its own model and metric.

Task A · Malware family classification (PE binary analysis)

Convolutional neural network operating on byte-level representations of portable executable files combined with PE-header structural features. The backbone is a 1D-CNN with residual blocks. Training data: EMBER 2018 dataset (1.1 million PE samples, 2,351 malware families) supplemented with customer-specific malware corpus where available. Expected performance on EMBER-held-out validation: 97-98% accuracy at multi-class family identification, consistent with published baselines.

Task B · Phishing URL detection

A gradient-boosted decision tree classifier on engineered URL features (length, TLD reputation, character-distribution entropy, path-component patterns, redirect chain analysis) combined with a character-level LSTM embedding. Training data: PhishTank verified phishing URLs, OpenPhish feeds, APWG samples, and benign URLs sampled from Common Crawl and customer traffic. Expected performance on held-out test: ROC-AUC 0.97–0.99 depending on phishing campaign sophistication.

Task C · Attack technique categorisation (ATT&CK)

A transformer-based classifier mapping endpoint telemetry sequences to MITRE ATT&CK technique IDs. The model takes a sequence of annotated events (process creation, file access, network connection, registry modification) and emits probabilities over the ATT&CK technique taxonomy. Training data: Atomic Red Team test outputs, MITRE CALDERA emulation traces, Endgame / Elastic endpoint data where available.

Task D · Alert severity classification

A gradient-boosted classifier that predicts severity level (informational / low / medium / high / critical) for a given alert based on alert metadata, asset criticality context, and contextual threat intelligence. Trained on historic customer triage adjudications and continuously refined through analyst feedback loop in ForgeSOC.

Retraining cadence

Classification models retrain quarterly in production with formal validation gating: challenger model must beat champion model by at least 2 percentage points on held-out test, and must pass adversarial robustness evaluation, before replacing the production model. NLP components retrain monthly as new phishing campaigns and threat-intelligence items arrive.

NLP / Threat Intelligence · detailed architecture
LLM-based processing of unstructured security text

Open-source large language models form the foundation of our threat-intelligence processing pipeline. We do not train foundation models from scratch — that would be economically irrational and scientifically unnecessary. We deploy proven open-source foundations (LLaMA 3 variants, Qwen 2 variants) with lightweight Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) fine-tuning for security-domain tasks, combined with retrieval-augmented generation for accuracy-critical workflows.

Base models and fine-tuning

  • LLaMA 3.1 8B-Instruct with LoRA adapters fine-tuned on security-domain corpora: CVE descriptions, MITRE ATT&CK technique descriptions, annotated threat-intelligence reports, phishing email samples with expert labelling. Used for the majority of threat-intelligence summarisation and extraction workflows where latency matters.
  • Qwen 2 7B-Instruct for workflows requiring longer context windows (32K tokens) including full threat-intelligence report ingestion and multi-document cross-referencing.
  • Qwen 2 72B-Instruct-Int4 for workflows requiring higher reasoning quality where latency tolerance is measured in seconds rather than milliseconds. Run in 4-bit quantised mode on our DDR5-ECC-equipped Xeon servers (approximately 1.5 tokens per second generation speed on CPU) or in async batch mode.

Retrieval-augmented generation architecture

For workflows where factual accuracy matters more than generation fluency — indicator-of-compromise extraction, CVE-to-environment mapping, attack-pattern matching — we use retrieval-augmented generation. The knowledge base includes:

  • Full MITRE ATT&CK (Enterprise, Mobile, ICS) technique and procedure descriptions.
  • NVD (National Vulnerability Database) CVE records updated daily.
  • CISA Known Exploited Vulnerabilities (KEV) catalog updated continuously.
  • Customer-specific asset inventory and configuration baselines.
  • Customer historical incident records (with analyst adjudication).

Documents are chunked, embedded with BGE-large-en-v1.5, indexed in a customer-isolated vector store (Qdrant or Weaviate depending on deployment), and retrieved via hybrid BM25-plus-dense-retrieval scoring. Top retrieved passages are composed into prompts with explicit attribution and passed to the LLM for generation with citation requirements.

Hallucination control

Hallucination is a known risk with LLM-based systems and is unacceptable in security workflows where false information can mislead incident response. Our mitigations:

  • Constrained generation. Critical outputs (IOC extraction, CVE identification) are constrained via JSON-schema output validation. Non-conforming outputs are rejected and regenerated or escalated to human review.
  • Grounded-only generation for critical paths. RAG outputs required to cite retrieved passages. Un-grounded claims flagged and suppressed.
  • Confidence thresholds. LLM-derived indicators below 0.85 confidence score blocked from automated action; human-in-loop required.
  • Parallel orthogonal validation. Classification tasks also served by gradient-boosted classifiers; LLM output discarded when disagreement with classical model exceeds calibrated threshold.

Correlation Engine · detailed architecture
Multi-source alert stitching into coherent incident narratives

The correlation engine is where AEGIS transitions from per-signal detection to multi-signal reasoning. A single unusual outbound connection may or may not be consequential; the same connection correlated with a specific user's authentication from a new geolocation and a specific host's recent registry modification becomes a high-confidence compromise hypothesis. The correlation engine is the component that performs this reasoning systematically and at scale.

Architecture

The correlation engine is a hybrid rule-plus-ML architecture:

  • Deterministic rule layer. A domain-specific language allows detection engineers to express high-confidence correlation rules (for example: "IF process injection alert AND outbound connection to known C2 IP within 5 minutes ON same host AND no approved administrative activity THEN raise compromise case"). Rules are authored by SOC analysts and reviewed for correctness.
  • Graph neural network layer. A graph neural network operates over an entity graph (hosts, users, processes, files, IPs, domains) with edges representing observed interactions. Messages propagate from high-confidence malicious nodes to identify likely compromised neighbours. GraphSAGE architecture with mean-pool aggregation.
  • Learned weight layer. A gradient-boosted model weights the rule-layer outputs and GNN-layer outputs against historical outcome data (true positive, false positive, benign noise) to produce final incident scoring.

Entity resolution

Cross-telemetry correlation requires reliable entity resolution: is the user "jane.doe@customer.com" in the authentication log the same entity as "JDOE\\CUSTOMER" in the endpoint log? We implement a combination of deterministic matching rules (email-UPN-SID mapping via identity-provider integration) and embedding-based similarity matching for edge cases. Customer-provided identity mappings override automated inference.

Case lifecycle

The correlation engine produces cases rather than individual alerts. A case represents a coherent hypothesis about a potential compromise, supported by the correlated alert evidence. Cases have a defined lifecycle: open → triaging → investigating → contained → remediated → closed-resolved or closed-false-positive. Analyst adjudications at closure feed back into the learned-weight layer training for continuous improvement.

05
Behavioral Analytics (UEBA) · detailed architecture
User and entity behavioral baselines with continuous update

User and entity behavior analytics (UEBA) is the AEGIS component targeting insider threat, compromised credential use, and "living-off-the-land" attacker techniques that evade signature-based detection. UEBA models attempt to detect deviations from normal behavior at the identity level.

Feature engineering

Per-user feature vectors are constructed from multiple telemetry sources including authentication logs (time, location, authentication method, success/failure), access logs (systems accessed, command patterns, data volumes), network activity (destinations, protocols, bytes transferred), and endpoint events (process patterns, file access patterns, privilege-escalation attempts). Features are aggregated at multiple time granularities (hour, day, week, month).

Baseline modeling

For each user, we maintain two parallel baseline models:

  • Gaussian mixture model over low-dimensional feature aggregates capturing the typical multi-modal nature of user activity (for example: "normal work hours", "after-hours critical system access", "weekend maintenance window"). New observations scored against the mixture; low log-probability indicates anomaly.
  • LSTM sequence model over the sequence of user events at fine granularity. The next-event-probability under the learned sequence model gives a fine-grained anomaly score that catches patterns the GMM would miss.

Peer-group baselines

Beyond per-user baselines, we also maintain peer-group baselines. A user whose behavior deviates from their own history is suspicious; a user whose behavior deviates from their peer group (same role, same department, same team) when their own history is recent or limited is equally suspicious. Peer groups are derived from identity-provider group memberships, role-based access control profiles, and human-resource-information-system integrations where provided.

Handling privacy-sensitive behavioral data

UEBA inherently processes employee behavioral data, which raises substantive privacy concerns. Our operational posture:

  • Feature extraction operates on aggregated behavioral signatures, not on raw content (for example: volume of data accessed, not the specific records).
  • All UEBA outputs include explanation ("alert raised because login from unusual geolocation at unusual time with unusual data-access volume") so that analyst and employee both understand the reasoning.
  • Customer explicitly controls which employees are enrolled in UEBA monitoring and which are excluded (typically executives and legal).
  • Deployment supports compliance with customer's internal employee monitoring disclosure requirements.

Predictive Vulnerability Intelligence · detailed architecture
Exploit-probability prediction for prioritisation

Most enterprises run continuous vulnerability scanning that identifies thousands of vulnerabilities per scan cycle. The economic problem is not identification; it is prioritisation. Which of the 14,000 open CVEs across the environment is the one most likely to be exploited before the next patch cycle? The predictive model addresses this question with machine learning.

Architecture

  • Gradient-boosted classifier (XGBoost / LightGBM) predicting the probability of in-the-wild exploitation within 30-day, 60-day, and 90-day horizons for each open CVE in the customer environment.
  • Feature engineering incorporating CVSS 3.1 vector components, NVD metadata, CWE taxonomy position, presence in exploit databases (Exploit-DB, Metasploit, GitHub proof-of-concept repos), CISA Known Exploited Vulnerabilities status, and threat-actor attribution from threat-intelligence feeds.
  • Environment context layers customer-specific asset criticality, attack-surface exposure (internet-facing or internal-only), compensating control presence, and business-function dependency.
  • Temporal features capturing recency of disclosure, velocity of social-media and security-researcher discussion, and proximity to patch-Tuesday cycles.

Training data

Training labels derived from the Exploit Prediction Scoring System (EPSS) from FIRST.org, augmented with CISA KEV catalog, Mandiant advisories, and internal observations of exploit campaigns against customer environments. EPSS provides a well-calibrated baseline; our model adds environment-contextual features that raise or lower individual CVE priorities based on the specific customer's exposure.

Output and integration

The model outputs a prioritised patch list with explicit probability-of-exploitation estimates, expected business impact if exploited, and recommended remediation timeline. Integrated into ForgeVault for cloud security posture management workflows, into ForgeSOC for incident-response prioritisation, and into ForgeIntel for threat- intelligence analyst workflow.

01
ForgeShield · Fraud & AML detail
Real-time scoring at sub-50-millisecond latency for Indian BFSI

ForgeShield is the AlgoForge product targeting the largest dedicated security-technology budget line in Indian banking: fraud detection and anti-money-laundering. Every scheduled commercial bank, payments bank, and payments aggregator in India is required by Reserve Bank of India mandate to operate a twenty-four-by-seven fraud monitoring capability with early detection, incident reporting to RBI within prescribed timelines, and continuous improvement demonstration. ForgeShield addresses the detection, scoring, and analyst- workflow layers of this requirement.

Real-time scoring architecture

The core fraud scoring pipeline is designed for sub-50-millisecond p99 latency on transaction scoring. This is the latency envelope required for UPI, IMPS, and card-present transaction authorisation where a delay of even 100 milliseconds creates customer experience degradation. The architecture uses a precomputed-feature-store model: transaction-relevant features (velocity counters, counterparty history, geographic consistency, device fingerprint age) are materialised in an in-memory feature store (Redis or Apache Ignite depending on deployment scale) and updated asynchronously from the transaction event stream. At transaction time, scoring requires only feature lookup plus gradient-boosted-tree model inference plus graph neural network propagation for fraud-ring detection, all of which comfortably fit within the latency budget.

Graph neural network for fraud ring detection

Conventional fraud detection operates at the transaction level or at most the individual account level. Sophisticated fraud (first-party fraud rings, synthetic identity fraud, mule network fraud) operates at the network level: a set of accounts, devices, IP addresses, phone numbers, and counterparties that are interconnected in ways that only become apparent when viewed as a graph. ForgeShield implements a GraphSAGE-based graph neural network operating over the transaction- entity graph with minute-scale update latency. Fraud ring scores are surfaced alongside transaction scores and factor into the final risk decision.

AML typology coverage

Anti-money-laundering monitoring requires coverage of specific transaction patterns identified as indicative of money laundering by Financial Action Task Force (FATF) and the Indian Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU-IND). Our baseline typology coverage includes: structuring (smurfing), layering through shell accounts, rapid movement of funds, unusual geographic patterns, politically-exposed-person interaction patterns, and virtual-asset-service-provider interaction patterns. Typologies are implemented as a combination of deterministic rules (for auditability and regulator defensibility) and ML-enhanced scoring (for reduced false-positive rates).

Explainability for regulator defensibility

Every suspicious transaction alert surfaces an explanation of the reasoning: which features contributed to the score, what the feature values were at the transaction time, what the historical baseline for the same entity was, and whether any specific AML typology matched. This is not optional — RBI and FIU-IND both require that alerts raised for Suspicious Transaction Report filing have documented reasoning that can be reproduced during audit. SHAP values at the feature level plus rule matches at the typology level provide this reasoning automatically.

02
ForgeSentinel · Network Detection & Response
Deep network visibility without blind reliance on decryption

Network Detection and Response is the category of security product targeting detection of threats as they traverse the network: lateral movement after initial compromise, command-and-control communications, data exfiltration, policy violations by internal users. ForgeSentinel is architected for Indian BFSI deployments where packet-inspection budgets are substantial but decryption capabilities are regulatorily complex.

Deployment modes

  • Inline sensor deployed between network tier boundaries with wire-speed throughput at 10 / 25 / 40 Gigabit Ethernet rates depending on sensor class. Active mode supports optional inline blocking; passive mode operates at detection-only to preserve network performance.
  • SPAN port / TAP mirror for deployments where inline is not acceptable. Sensor processes copy of network traffic without injection of latency or failure risk into production path.
  • Virtual sensor for cloud environments via VPC flow log ingestion (AWS) / NSG flow log ingestion (Azure) / VPC flow log ingestion (GCP). Covers east-west traffic visibility without requiring inline cloud deployment.

Encrypted traffic analysis (without decryption)

Approximately ninety per cent of enterprise network traffic is TLS-encrypted. Decryption is operationally complex and regulatorily fraught. ForgeSentinel operates on TLS-metadata-based machine learning: JA3 / JA3S fingerprints of TLS client and server hellos, certificate chain features, packet-size and inter-arrival-time distributions, destination port and SNI history. This approach, documented by Anderson and McGrew (2017) and subsequent literature, achieves strong detection of malware command-and-control communications without requiring decryption.

Lateral movement detection

Once an attacker has gained a foothold in an enterprise network, lateral movement to high-value targets is typically the next phase. ForgeSentinel maintains a model of "normal" east-west traffic patterns: which servers normally communicate with which workstations, at what volume, using which protocols. Deviations from this baseline — a workstation initiating RDP to multiple servers in sequence, a server-to-server connection using SMB that has never occurred before, PowerShell Remoting from an unusual source — are surfaced as lateral-movement candidates for correlation- engine processing.

ForgeIdentity · Zero Trust Architecture
Identity-first security with risk-based continuous authentication

ForgeIdentity is the Zero Trust Architecture product focusing on identity as the primary security control plane. As perimeter-based security becomes inadequate for hybrid-cloud and remote-workforce environments, identity-first Zero Trust patterns have emerged as the prevailing architectural approach.

Continuous risk-based authentication

Rather than single authentication at session start, ForgeIdentity continuously evaluates risk throughout the user session. Risk factors include: device posture, geolocation consistency, behavioral pattern (from UEBA model), resource sensitivity, session age, multi-factor authentication recency. When risk crosses threshold, the session is stepped up (re-authentication required), limited (access to specific resources restricted), or terminated.

Identity threat detection

Specific identity-layer attacks are detected: password spraying (low-volume authentication attempts across many accounts), credential stuffing (volume of attempts against a single account), impossible-travel geolocation patterns, golden-ticket attacks (anomalous Kerberos tickets in AD environments), SAML-response manipulation, OAuth-token theft.

Privileged access workflow

Privileged account activity (administrative operations, database access, production deployment) is subject to enhanced monitoring: session recording, command auditing, ticket-based justification requirement, approval-chain workflow for high-impact operations. Integrates with existing PAM solutions where customers have them deployed.

Model Governance

Every ML model deployed to BFSI customers passes six gates.

ML model governance is the aspect of AI-native cybersecurity that most distinguishes production-grade platforms from prototype-grade platforms. A model that shows impressive performance on curated test data but has not been validated for adversarial robustness, explainability, drift handling, and regulator defensibility is not ready for BFSI deployment. The AEGIS engine operates under a formal model-governance framework applied uniformly across all six models.

Gate 01 · Training Quality
Dataset curation and labelling audit
Every training dataset documented for provenance, licensing, representativeness, and label noise. Held-out test set frozen before model development begins. Data pipeline versioning via DVC or equivalent.
Gate 02 · Performance
Multi-metric performance validation
Candidate model must beat champion on primary metric (typically precision at fixed recall or ROC-AUC) by calibrated margin, plus pass secondary-metric gates (calibration, subgroup performance, latency, memory footprint).
Gate 03 · Adversarial Robustness
PGD / FGSM / transfer attack testing
Every production model tested against Projected Gradient Descent and Fast Gradient Sign Method attacks at calibrated epsilon budgets. Robustness degradation must be below threshold; if not, model rejected or adversarial training applied.
Gate 04 · Explainability
SHAP / LIME attribution at alert level
Every alert or score must surface SHAP (preferred) or LIME attribution showing the contribution of each feature. Explanation must be audit-grade: reproducible, stable under minor perturbations, and interpretable by a domain expert.
Gate 05 · Drift Handling
Concept drift detection and retraining cadence
Deployed model instrumented with drift-detection algorithms (ADWIN, Page-Hinkley, population stability index). Drift above threshold triggers automated retraining pipeline; failure to recover performance escalates to ML team for intervention.
Gate 06 · Post-deployment Monitoring
Production metric tracking and incident process
Every production model instrumented with performance metrics (precision, recall, false positive rate at prod-time) and operational metrics (latency, error rate, resource consumption). Incident response playbook for model failure.
MLOps
MLOps infrastructure
The continuous-integration pipeline for ML models

Model governance is enforced not through manual process but through an engineered MLOps pipeline. Every model candidate proceeds through an automated six-gate evaluation before human reviewer approval and promotion to production.

  • Experiment tracking: Weights & Biases or MLflow tracking for every training run, with hyperparameters, dataset versions, metrics, and artifact persistence.
  • Model registry: MLflow Model Registry or equivalent, with model versions tagged by stage (staging, challenger, champion, archived).
  • CI/CD for models: Every model commit triggers automated training on staging dataset, evaluation against the six gates, and promotion to challenger status if passing. Human reviewer required for champion promotion.
  • Canary deployment: New champion models deployed to 1–5% of traffic for 48–72 hours under parallel shadow evaluation against retiring champion. Full rollout only after canary validation.
  • Rollback: One-click rollback to previous champion in case of production regression. Rollback triggers automatic incident ticket for ML team.

Stream 03 · Backup & Disaster Recovery

3-2-1-1-0 backup architecture. Tested DR playbooks.

Backup-as-a-Service and Disaster-Recovery-as-a-Service constitute a natural expansion stream for Indian enterprises running hybrid-cloud environments. The practical reality is that most mid-market enterprises have backup systems that technically exist but have never been tested at recovery, run with retention policies that no longer match regulatory requirements, and cover historical assets rather than current cloud footprints. Modernising this is a concrete, immediate-value service.

B
Backup-as-a-Service architecture
  • 3-2-1-1-0 principle: three copies of data, on two different media types, with one copy offsite, one copy immutable (ransomware-resistant), and zero recovery errors verified through regular recovery testing.
  • Backup software: Veeam Backup & Replication or Commvault Complete Backup & Recovery for enterprise Windows/Linux workloads; custom backup-tooling for cloud-native Kubernetes workloads (Velero).
  • Coverage: physical servers, virtual machines (VMware, Hyper-V, KVM, Proxmox), Microsoft 365 tenants, Google Workspace tenants, Kubernetes persistent volumes, database-native backups, file-server shares.
  • Encryption: AES-256 encryption at rest with customer-managed keys held in hardware security module; client-side encryption prior to transit so even we cannot read backup contents.
  • Immutable storage: write-once-read-many policy on backup repositories to prevent ransomware from encrypting backups.
  • Retention policies: customisable per backup set, with defaults aligned to common regulatory expectations (7-year retention for financial-record workloads, 5-year for healthcare, 3-year for general business).
D
Disaster-Recovery-as-a-Service
  • Pilot-light DR: minimal always-on infrastructure in DR site, scaled up on disaster declaration. Lowest ongoing cost; recovery time objective (RTO) typically 4–8 hours.
  • Warm-standby DR: scaled-down but running replica of production in DR site with continuous data replication. RTO typically 30–60 minutes, recovery point objective (RPO) typically 5–15 minutes.
  • Hot-standby DR: full-scale running replica with active-passive or active-active load distribution. RTO sub-minute, RPO near-zero. Highest cost; used for tier-1 workloads.
  • Replication technology: asynchronous storage replication, database-native replication (PostgreSQL streaming replication, MySQL GTID-based replication), or hypervisor-level replication (Zerto, VMware SRM).
  • DR runbook documentation: every customer DR plan documented with ordered-step runbook, role assignments, communication plan, escalation path.
  • Quarterly DR testing: scheduled DR exercise verifying that recovery runbooks execute as documented. Discrepancies tracked as findings with remediation timeline.
C
Compliance and assurance

  • RBI IS Audit compliance: backup procedures, retention policies, and recovery testing documented in form suitable for RBI Information Systems Audit evidence.
  • Ransomware-specific protection: immutable backup repositories, air-gapped backup copies, and detection of unusual backup-encryption patterns that could indicate ransomware activity.

Why Sapphire Rapids matters for our workloads
AMX, AVX-512, VNNI — accelerated ML inference on CPU

The Sapphire Rapids generation of Xeon processors is particularly well-suited to our workload mix for several specific reasons:

  • AMX (Advanced Matrix Extensions): dedicated silicon for matrix multiply-accumulate operations that accelerates neural network inference substantially for INT8 and BF16 workloads. For quantised LLM inference (INT8 or INT4) and for embedding computation, AMX provides materially higher throughput than prior-generation AVX-512 alone.
  • AVX-512 with VNNI: Vector Neural Network Instructions accelerate INT8 inference even without invoking AMX. Important for workloads where AMX programming models are not yet supported (older framework versions, custom operators).
  • PCIe 5.0: doubles available bandwidth per lane compared to PCIe 4.0, reducing GPU-to-CPU data-transfer bottlenecks for training workloads.
  • Eight memory channels per socket: delivers substantially higher memory bandwidth than previous six-channel designs, particularly valuable for memory-bound workloads (in-memory databases, large-context LLM inference).
  • DDR5 at 4800 MT/s: approximately 60% higher memory bandwidth than DDR4 at 3200 MT/s, with larger per-DIMM capacities enabling 1.4 TB total memory on each server.

For quantised LLM inference specifically, AMX-equipped Sapphire Rapids platforms deliver 2–4× the throughput of Ice Lake-based predecessors on equivalent workloads, according to Intel's published benchmarks and third-party validation. This places CPU inference within a reasonable performance envelope for many non-latency-critical workloads, reducing GPU dependency.

Research & Publications

Scientific methodology as competitive moat.

Quisitive is being built to last. A
Research agenda — Bioinformatics

Over Year 1 to Year 3, our Bioinformatics research agenda addresses three specific technical challenges where we believe we can contribute incrementally to the community while improving our service delivery:

  • Indian-specific ADMET benchmark datasets. Most published ADMET benchmarks derive from Western pharmaceutical company datasets and western clinical populations. Indian-population pharmacogenomic and metabolic profiles differ in meaningful ways (cytochrome P450 polymorphism frequencies, bioavailability variance by ethnic background). We plan to curate and publish an Indian-annotated extension to public ADMET benchmarks, in collaboration with Indian academic partners.
  • Uncertainty quantification for graph-neural-network property prediction. Current ADMET prediction models typically output point estimates. Operational use requires uncertainty estimates to decide when to trust the prediction versus escalate to wet-lab validation. We are investigating deep-ensemble and evidential- learning approaches for calibrated uncertainty.
  • Natural-product scaffold-aware virtual screening. Indian pharmaceutical research has a long tradition of natural-product-inspired drug discovery. Standard virtual-screening pipelines are tuned for synthetic-chemistry libraries. We plan methodology improvements for natural-product scaffolds using ChEMBL NPs and COCONUT databases.
B
Research agenda — Cybersecurity

The cybersecurity research agenda focuses on challenges specific to the Indian BFSI threat landscape where published Western research is often imperfect as-is:

  • UPI fraud detection benchmark. Unified Payments Interface fraud patterns are distinctive from Western card-fraud patterns (different velocities, different counterparty networks, different linguistic artefacts). We plan to publish an anonymised UPI fraud detection benchmark in partnership with customer institutions willing to contribute de-identified data.
  • Hindi and Indian-language phishing detection. Multi-lingual phishing campaigns targeting Indian banking customers combine English, Hindi, and regional-language content. Published phishing benchmarks are English-dominant. We plan to contribute multi-lingual detection benchmarks to the community.
  • Explainability for BFSI regulatory contexts. Reserve Bank of India regulatory audit requirements for AI-derived alerts create specific explainability needs. We plan research publications on SHAP-plus-counterfactual explainability frameworks suited to BFSI audit reports.

S
Scientific integrity framework
  • Reproducibility. Every publication includes full methodology documentation, code availability (GitHub), and data availability where licencing permits.
  • Benchmark-first evaluation. Every methodology claim is evaluated on public benchmarks with published numbers; proprietary benchmarks only when justified.

  • Author credit fairly allocated. Every contributor of substantive technical work is named co-author. External collaborators (academic partners, SAB members) credited appropriately.
  • No marketing-driven science. Benchmark selection and result reporting follow scientific standards, not commercial convenience.

  • Scientific or engineering depth. We prefer candidates who have done the thing they claim to know, not candidates who have talked about it. Employee Stock Option Programme

How our ESOP works.

The ESOP is structured to be meaningful ownership for every employee, not a decorative line item. Below is the operational summary of how the programme runs. Specific grant sizes are role-calibrated and communicated explicitly in offer letters.

ESOP mechanics
Structure, vesting, exercise
  • ESOP pool size. 12% pool established at incorporation. Diluted pro-rata through each investor round; residual pool at Phase 3 close approximately 7–8%.
  • Eligibility. All full-time employees from the day of confirmation-of-employment. Contractors and consultants are not eligible.
  • Vesting. 4-year vesting with 1-year cliff. Monthly vesting after cliff. Standard double-trigger acceleration in change-of-control scenarios for senior employees.

  • Tax handling. ESOP taxation follows current Indian Income Tax Act provisions — perquisite tax at exercise (on FMV over strike), capital gains at eventual sale. We assist with tax clarification but do not provide personal tax advice.
  • Secondary liquidity. Structured secondary opportunities (tender offers, company buyback) evaluated at Series A and in preparation for later liquidity events. Not guaranteed but actively considered.

  • Grant bands by role level published internally and communicated to candidates during offer discussions. We don’t negotiate grant size heavily during offer; we aim to make first offers that reflect the role.
  • Compensation committee (in place from Phase 2) reviews all senior-role grants. Consists of 1 investor director + 1 founder director + 1 independent director.
  • Refreshers. Annual refresher grants considered for strong-performing employees to align ongoing vested-equity with continued contribution.
  • Recognition grants. One-time additional grants for exceptional contribution (major customer delivery, major scientific publication, major product milestone). Reviewed by compensation committee.
  • Transparent programme documentation. Full ESOP scheme document shared with every participating employee. Annual update letter summarising pool utilisation, dilution, and individual vested positions.

Company & Strategy

The high-level questions about who we are and why.

Why three verticals instead of focusing on one?

The shared foundation — Rs 3.85 Cr of deployed HPC infrastructure, senior engineering discipline, regulatory-grade security posture, and back-office — makes carrying three verticals significantly cheaper than building three standalone companies. Each vertical would need its own infrastructure, its own compliance programme, its own CFO stack. We build those once.

The verticals also reinforce each other commercially: Managed Services generates cashflow from Month 6 to de-risk the longer-cycle Bioinformatics and Cybersecurity ramps. Cybersecurity’s BFSI compliance stack is reusable by every customer on our platform. Bioinformatics establishes scientific credibility that compounds across verticals.

That said, we are explicit that each vertical must stand on its own unit economics. We do not subsidise one vertical’s CAC from another’s margin. The holding-company structure is a capital-efficiency choice, not a hiding place for weak economics.

What makes Quisitive different from Indian CRDMOs, MSSPs, or cloud providers?

Indian CRDMOs (Syngene, Biocon) are wet-lab-first with computational services as a supporting layer. We are computational-first, designed for the customers who want in-silico work done before they commit wet-lab budget. Indian MSSPs largely resell global vendors (Palo Alto, Fortinet, CrowdStrike) with analyst labour on top. Our Cybersecurity vertical is first-party AI-product-led with our own AEGIS models. Indian cloud providers (Tata, NxtGen, Yotta) are IaaS-first. Our Managed Services vertical pairs IaaS with AI-inference, managed security, and cross-vertical workload integration.

Most importantly: no single Indian player today combines all three. That intersection is where we position. See the Team & SAB page for the human capability that makes this feasible, and the Infrastructure page for the technical posture.

What kinds of drug-discovery projects can you take on?

We support the computational portion of early-stage discovery: target identification and validation (literature mining, druggability analysis, homology modelling when no experimental structure exists), virtual screening against commercial or customer-provided libraries, ADMET profiling (hERG, CYP450, BBB, solubility, metabolic stability), molecular-dynamics validation of top hits, and lead-optimisation workflows including free-energy-perturbation (FEP+) in Year 2 onward.

What tooling do you use and what licensing does that imply?

The core toolchain is peer-reviewed open-source: RDKit (BSD), AutoDock Vina and GNINA, GROMACS and OpenMM (LGPL / permissive), DeepChem and ChemProp, PyTorch, PyTorch Geometric, ESMFold (MIT). How do you protect our chemical IP when you run our libraries?

Per-customer infrastructure pods with isolated compute, storage, and network namespaces; per-customer encryption keys; NDAs executed before any data transfer; operational logs retained for audit but sanitised to exclude customer-specific chemical identifiers where operationally feasible. We do not use customer chemical data to train shared models across customers. Data-retention policy is aligned to the contract term — post-engagement data is cryptographically destroyed after a documented retention period.

Not at launch. Cybersecurity

Questions from CISOs, SOC managers, and procurement teams.

What does AEGIS actually do, in one paragraph?

AEGIS is our in-house AI engine composed of six production machine-learning models: anomaly detection (VAE + isolation forest + LSTM), multi-task classification (malware, phishing, MITRE ATT&CK mapping, alert severity), NLP / threat intelligence (LoRA-adapted open-weight LLMs with RAG), correlation engine (rule DSL + GraphSAGE GNN), user/entity behavioural analytics (GMM + LSTM sequence), and predictive vulnerability prioritisation (XGBoost on CVSS + NVD + KEV + EPSS features). The six AlgoForge products (ForgeShield, ForgeSentinel, ForgeVault, ForgeSOC, ForgeIntel, ForgeIdentity) consume AEGIS models via internal gRPC/REST services.

How does AEGIS handle adversarial ML attacks?

Every AEGIS model promotion pipeline includes adversarial robustness testing (projected gradient descent, fast gradient sign method) and drift detection. Model retraining cadence is tuned to drift rate: NLP / threat-intel weekly, anomaly / behavioural monthly, classification / predictive quarterly. All model outputs carry confidence scores; automated action is gated to high-confidence outputs only. Human-in-the-loop review is required for LLM-derived indicators of compromise before action. These controls are specifically designed for BFSI-audit defensibility.

Can BFSI auditors get evidence that your models are not a black box?

Yes. Every model carries model-governance documentation: training dataset provenance, feature inventory, fairness-and-bias assessment where applicable, SHAP / LIME explainability surface for specific predictions, confidence distribution, adversarial-robustness test results, retraining log. For BFSI customers we provide this documentation under NDA as part of the procurement cycle. An RBI-inspection-equivalent walk-through of model decisioning for any specific alert is possible through the platform’s audit-trail surfaces.

How do AlgoForge products integrate with my existing SIEM / XDR stack?

Primary integration patterns: (a) bidirectional API integration with Splunk, Elastic, Microsoft Sentinel, IBM QRadar via standardised connectors; (b) SOAR-compatible webhooks for Palo Alto XSOAR, Swimlane, Tines; (c) CEF/LEEF log forwarding for enrichment workflows; (d) STIX/TAXII 2.1 for threat-intelligence exchange. We publish an integration catalogue as part of the procurement pack. For customers running proprietary SIEMs we evaluate custom integration on a case-by-case basis.

How does your SOC-analyst cost-saving claim map to reality?

Industry benchmarks (Radiant Security, Dropzone AI, Gartner 2026) show AI-augmented SOCs free 80–90% of L1 triage workload and 30–60% of L2 investigation workload — combined 55–75%. Customers typically redeploy that freed capacity rather than reducing headcount: the actual realised headcount reduction we observe in industry data is 20–35%. We price our ForgeSOC product to deliver net positive customer economics even at the lower end of realised savings.

Can I deploy AEGIS / AlgoForge on-premise in my own DC?

Customer-deployable architecture is on the roadmap for Phase 2 / Year 2. Through Year 1 the primary deployment model is managed-hosted from our Greater Noida DC, with dedicated customer pods for data isolation and network-segmentation. Customer-on-premise deployment adds operational complexity we want to validate in shorter cycles first. Hybrid deployments (model inference at customer DC with centralised management plane) are evaluated for specific BFSI customers on a case-by-case basis.

How do you handle false-positive tuning for new customers?

Per-customer tuning during a 30–60 day baseline-learning period. Anomaly models learn customer-environment normals before emitting alerts at production confidence levels; UEBA peer-group baselines establish behavioural norms on customer identity and device populations; correlation rule weights are calibrated to customer alert-tolerance. Post-baseline, we target false-positive rates at or below published industry SOC benchmarks (<3% of alerts escalated to L2 being confirmed benign on review).

Managed Services

Why would I pick your private cloud over AWS / Azure / GCP India?

Three factors: cost, egress, and sovereignty. For steady-state workloads with predictable capacity, on-premise HPC is materially cheaper than cloud list prices once reserved-pricing and egress are factored in. Indian cloud-egress charges of Rs 7–8/GB punish cloud-native Indian SaaS at scale. And for customers who need documented in-India data residency with customer-controlled key management, our on-premise posture is architecturally simpler to audit than cloud equivalents.

We are equally clear on where we are not the right choice: globally-distributed workloads, workloads with unpredictable burst capacity requirements above our current rack size, and workloads that need global- CDN integration. Those remain cloud’s strengths and we will say so.

What does “Private AIaaS” actually mean in practice?

Private, dedicated-inference endpoints for open-weight foundation models (LLaMA 3 family, Qwen 2 family, Mistral, Mixtral) and embedding models (BGE, E5).
